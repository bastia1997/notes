Financial Literacy

From what money actually is, all the way to pricing an option with Black-Scholes. A long, ordered path, in English, covering banks, mortgages, bonds, stocks, derivatives, pension funds, taxes, crypto, real estate and financial crises. University-grade material made chewable: every concept comes with intuition, formula, a worked numerical example, and — where it matters — a warning about how you can be ripped off.

How to use

Sections follow a logical order: each chapter builds on the previous. You can jump to what you need (e.g. straight to "Mortgages" if you're about to buy a house), but if you want to actually understand why a floating-rate mortgage can wreck you, read the central-bank section first. Every page contains:

  • Theory — the economic/financial "why" behind the product, with academic references where they help.
  • Concrete numbers — examples in euros/dollars, realistic percentages, step-by-step calculations. Formulas rendered with KaTeX.
  • Charts — Mermaid diagrams and inline SVG, no ASCII art.
  • Exercises and scenarios — "what happens if…", questions with hidden solutions, portfolio simulations.
  • Traps and frauds — where sales people fleece you and how to spot it.

Philosophy

Financial literacy is not about "getting rich". It is about understanding the contracts you already sign: the mortgage, the life insurance, the pension fund, the hidden fees of your advisor. The gap between someone who understands and someone who doesn't, over a working life, easily reaches hundreds of thousands of euros. Not because the literate one "picks the right stock" — that's near-impossible — but because they avoid the big mistakes: the fund with a 2.5% annual TER, the index-linked policy sold as "safe investment", the floating-rate mortgage taken at the rate peak.

No "double your money in a year" promises. No trading signals. No sponsorships. Just the conceptual machinery that lets you read a KID (Key Information Document) and understand what you are buying. The rest is details — important, but details.

Who it's for

  • Anyone starting from zero who wants an ordered path without skipping fundamentals.
  • Anyone who knows a piece (e.g. "I invest in ETFs") but has big gaps (e.g. "I have no idea what bond duration is").
  • Anyone about to make a concrete decision (mortgage, pension fund, recurring investment plan) who wants to understand before signing.
  • Anyone preparing for certifications like CFA Level I/II, EFPA EFA/EFP, or university exams in Financial Intermediation.

Who it's NOT for

  • Anyone looking for "the secret to easy money". It doesn't exist, and whoever sells it to you is selling you something else.
  • Anyone who only wants trading signals or "stock picks". There are none here. Pages on technical analysis also explain why academic evidence is skeptical.

Recommended order

Follow sections in order. Estimated time for the full path: 3–6 months at 30–60 minutes per day. It's not a race. The "Reference" chapters (bilingual A-Z glossary and formula sheet) are meant to be consulted back and forth at will.
