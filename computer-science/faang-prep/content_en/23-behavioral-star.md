# Behavioral interview

**The most underrated round.** Many devs think it's enough to "be themselves". Wrong.

Big Tech (especially **Amazon**, but also Google, Meta, Microsoft) have a **structured evaluation grid**. Those who prepare win.

## Part 1 — Why behavioral matters

### What they really measure

Technical rounds verify "can they solve problems?". Behavioral verifies:

1. **Communication**: do they explain clearly what they did?
2. **Ownership**: do they take responsibility or blame others?
3. **Collaboration**: do they handle conflict maturely?
4. **Self-awareness**: do they recognize their mistakes?
5. **Impact**: were their contributions significant?
6. **Cultural fit**: do they embody the company's values?

In big companies, **a bad employee is more expensive than a good one slipping through**. Behavioral is the main filter.

### How much it weighs

- Amazon: ~50% of total loop weight.
- Google: 30-40%.
- Meta ("Jedi" specific): 25-30%.
- Senior+: even more (team management).

### What you must know

- **STAR framework**.
- **Your 10 main stories**.
- **The specific company's principles** (Amazon LP, Google's "Googliness", Meta values).
- **Questions to ask YOU back to the interviewer**.

## Part 2 — The STAR framework

Acronym to structure every answer:

- **S**ituation: context. 1-2 sentences. *"Last year I worked on a project X at Y."*
- **T**ask: specific role. What did you have to do? *"I was responsible for migrating the legacy DB."*
- **A**ction: what **you** did. The longest part (60% of the time). Specific, detailed.
- **R**esult: measurable outcome + lesson learned.

### Killer mistakes

#### 1. Using "we" instead of "I"

The interviewer evaluates **you**, not the team. Clearly distinguish.

**Bad**: *"We improved latency."*

**Good**: *"I identified the bottleneck in the query, I rewrote the index, I measured a 40% improvement."*

#### 2. Skipping Result or making it vague

**Bad**: *"The project went well."*

**Good**: *"We reduced response times by 65%, from 1.2s to 420ms. Churn dropped 8% in the following months."*

#### 3. Vague Action

**Bad**: *"I coordinated the team."*

**Good**: *"I organized weekly sync with the 3 PMs, I created a Notion with critical bug status, I prioritized based on impact × effort."*

#### 4. Story too long

> 3 minutes = you lose attention.

Practice: time it. A well-told STAR story is **2-3 minutes**.

## Part 3 — The 10 stories to prepare

Map your experience on these 10 dimensions. A single story can cover 2-3 dimensions. You need **7-10 total stories**.

| # | Dimension | Example prompt |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Hardest technical problem | "Tell me about the hardest bug you debugged" |
| 2 | Conflict with coworker | "Tell me about a disagreement with a teammate" |
| 3 | Conflict with manager | "Tell me about a time you disagreed with your boss" |
| 4 | Failure / mistake | "Tell me about a time you failed" |
| 5 | Project owned end-to-end | "Tell me about a project you led from start to finish" |
| 6 | Tight deadline / pressure | "Time you had to ship under tight deadline" |
| 7 | Mentored / helped someone | "Time you mentored a junior" |
| 8 | Influenced without authority | "Convince someone to adopt your idea" |
| 9 | Learning something new | "Last time you learned a new technology" |
| 10 | Customer obsession / user impact | "Time you delivered value to customer/user" |

### Preparation workflow

1. **Write each story** in full version (200-300 words).
2. **Reduce to 90 seconds** ("elevator" version).
3. **Practice out loud**. Record yourself. Brutal but effective.
4. **Add numbers**: times, percentages, costs. Quantify.
5. **Add lesson learned**: what did you learn? Show growth.

## Part 4 — Amazon Leadership Principles (16)

Amazon evaluates ONLY on these. Every question is marked with one or more LPs. For the Amazon loop you need **at least 12-15 stories**.

### 1. Customer Obsession

"Start with the customer and work backwards."

Stories: a case where you flipped a technical choice to improve user experience. Did you talk directly with customers?

### 2. Ownership

"Leaders are owners. They think long term. They never say 'that's not my job'."

Stories: did you take responsibility for a problem outside your job description? Did you think beyond the next sprint?

### 3. Invent and Simplify

"They expect and require innovation and invention from their teams."

Stories: did you invent something new? Did you simplify a complex process?

### 4. Are Right, A Lot

"Strong business judgment and good instincts."

Stories: did you make a decision against common opinion that turned out right? Also know how to give calibration examples (changing opinion in light of data).

### 5. Learn and Be Curious

Stories: did you learn a new technology aggressively? Did you ask why of something others took for granted?

### 6. Hire and Develop the Best

For roles with management. Did you hire someone? Did you develop a junior into senior?

### 7. Insist on the Highest Standards

"Continually raise the bar."

Stories: did you refuse to accept a mediocre fix? Did you impose stricter code reviews?

### 8. Think Big

"Communicate a bold direction that inspires results."

Stories: did you propose a bold vision? Did you think 10x bigger than requested?

### 9. Bias for Action

"Speed matters. Many decisions are reversible."

Stories: did you ship something quickly to get feedback? 2-way door vs 1-way door decision.

### 10. Frugality

"Constraints breed resourcefulness, self-sufficiency, and invention."

Stories: did you achieve a result with fewer resources? Did you find a cheap solution?

### 11. Earn Trust

"Listen attentively, speak candidly, treat others respectfully."

Stories: did you tell an uncomfortable truth? Did you recover someone's trust?

### 12. Dive Deep

"Leaders operate at all levels. No task is beneath them."

Stories: did you dig into details of a system where others stopped at the surface? Did you investigate logs directly?

### 13. Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit

"Respectfully challenge decisions when they disagree, even when uncomfortable."

Stories: did you express disagreement with management/team constructively? Did you then fully commit to the decision made?

### 14. Deliver Results

Stories: did you deliver a difficult project, on time, with quality?

### 15-16. Best Earth's Employer / Broad Responsibility

Recent, soft. Less critical.

## Part 5 — Execution tactics

### 1. Specific > generic

Never *"I optimized an algorithm"*. Always *"I reduced from O(n²) to O(n log n) using external merge sort on a 50M-row dataset → time from 8h to 12 min → daily batch instead of weekly"*.

### 2. Always quantify

- Time: "reduced from 4h to 20 min".
- Cost: "$50K/month less".
- Users: "300K users impacted".
- Code: "removed 5000 lines of legacy".
- Adoption: "12 teams use the library".

If you don't have precise numbers, **honest orders of magnitude**.

### 3. Lesson learned (always)

Conclude with what you learned. **Even in success stories**. Shows growth.

### 4. Never speak ill of colleagues/managers

Even in conflict stories. Respect. Talk about process, not people.

*"We had different perspectives for valid reasons. He prioritized X, I prioritized Y."*

### 5. Never "I've never failed"

Unacceptable answer. Shows lack of self-awareness. Even a university project works.

### 6. Verify you answered

At the end: *"Does that answer your question? Want me to dive deeper into any part?"*

## Part 7 — Template "Tell me about yourself" (90 sec)

1. **Present** (20 sec): what you do now.
2. **Past** (30 sec): 1-2 career highlights.
3. **Future** (15 sec): why you're looking + why this company.
4. **Pitch** (15 sec): "I'm excited about this role because...".

Example (adapt to your profile):

> *"I'm a software engineer with 4 years of experience, currently at X working on Y where I've led the migration from B to C, reducing infra costs by 40%. Before that, I spent 2 years at A focusing on data pipelines. I'm looking for a new role because I want to work on Y at scale, and what excites me about [Company] is the opportunity to work on [specific product/problem]."*

## Part 8 — Questions to ask YOU to the interviewer (5-10 final min)

**ALWAYS have 3-4 ready**. Strong examples:

- *"What do you find most challenging about working here?"*
- *"How is success measured for this role in the first 6/12 months?"*
- *"What's the biggest engineering challenge the team faces right now?"*
- *"How does the team handle on-call?"*
- *"What's the team's relationship with product/design?"*
- *"What's been the most rewarding project you've worked on at [Company]?"*

**Avoid**: salary/benefits (recruiter); obvious things from the site; superficial questions ("How big is the team?" without follow-up).

## Part 9 — Complete example (with feedback)

> Q: "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a teammate."

### Weak answer

*"Once I disagreed with a colleague about architecture. We discussed and eventually found a compromise."*

❌ Generic, no specifics, no "I", no result, no lesson.

### Strong answer

**(Situation)** *"Last year I worked on a real-time notification service for 5M users. A senior colleague proposed using Kafka as the backbone, citing previous experience."*

**(Task)** *"I was responsible for the final technical choice. I felt Kafka was over-engineered for our use case, and that SQS would have been sufficient and easier to operate for a team without Kafka expertise."*

**(Action)** *"I did 3 things. First: I wrote a 2-page document comparing the two approaches on 5 dimensions: throughput, latency, cost, operational complexity, time-to-market. Second: I organized a 45-minute meeting where I presented it openly, inviting the colleague to challenge my numbers. Third: I proposed a compromise — start with SQS, but with an abstract interface that would let us migrate to Kafka if the numbers told us to."*

**(Result)** *"The team voted 4-1 in favor of my proposal. We shipped in 8 weeks instead of the estimated 16 for Kafka. After 6 months, SQS was handling our volumes without issues and we saved ~$30K/year in infra vs managed Kafka."*

**(Lesson)** *"Lesson: in technical disagreements, you must not approach them as 'who's right', but as 'what needs to be proven with data'. When I gave my colleague the opportunity to challenge numbers (not opinions), we found common ground."*

✓ Specific, quantified, clear "I", action articulated in 3 steps, measurable result, honest lesson.

## Summary

1. **Behavioral weighs 30-50% of the loop**. Prepare seriously.
2. **STAR**: Situation, Task, Action, Result. Action is 60%.
3. **10 main stories**, each in 90s + 3 minutes version.
4. **Amazon LP** (for Amazon): 14 to memorize with at least 1 story each.
5. **Always quantify**. Lesson learned at the end.
6. **Never speak ill of anyone**. Never "I never failed".
7. **Your questions to interviewer**: ALWAYS prepare 3-4.

Practice 50+ times out loud. Behavioral is the round where practice makes you invincible.
