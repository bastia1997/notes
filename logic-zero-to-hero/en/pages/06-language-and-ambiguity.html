Language, ambiguity, definitions

Words are noisy. Lexical, syntactic, and scope ambiguity. Five kinds of definitions. Vagueness vs ambiguity. Equivocation as the root of many fallacies.

Area
Language
Prerequisites
  • (none)
Tools / references
  • (none specific)

Logic operates on precise statements. Natural language doesn't deliver them. Before any argument can be analyzed, the words it uses must be pinned down.

1. Three types of ambiguity

1.1 Lexical

A single word has multiple meanings. Bank (financial institution / river edge). Light (illumination / not heavy). Resolved by context — usually.

1.2 Syntactic (structural)

The sentence structure allows multiple parsings.

"I saw the man with the telescope."

Did I have the telescope, or the man? Both parsings are grammatical. Famous Groucho Marx joke: "One morning I shot an elephant in my pajamas. How he got into my pajamas, I don't know."

1.3 Scope (quantifier)

"Everyone loves someone."

Means (a) every person has at least one beloved (∀x ∃y), or (b) there's one specific person everyone loves (∃y ∀x)? Very different claims.

2. Vagueness vs ambiguity

  • Ambiguity: discrete alternative meanings. Can be resolved by choosing one.
  • Vagueness: borderline cases without sharp boundary. Bald, tall, rich. Resists resolution (see Sorites paradox).

Both are problems for reasoning, but different problems.

3. Five kinds of definitions

Type What it does Example
Lexical reports common usage "Lion: a large carnivorous feline (Panthera leo)"
Stipulative introduces new meaning for a term "Let gizmo mean any object weighing 1–2 kg"
Ostensive by pointing "Red is the color of THAT object"
Genus + difference locate in class, then specify "Man = rational animal"
Operational by measurement procedure "Intelligence = score on the WAIS test"

Operational definitions are crucial in science: they make a term testable. But they also constrain — "intelligence-as-IQ-test" is a stipulation that drops much of ordinary meaning.

4. Equivocation: shifting meanings mid-argument

A classic informal fallacy. Same word used with two different meanings in the same argument.

"Nothing is better than eternal happiness. A ham sandwich is better than nothing. Therefore, a ham sandwich is better than eternal happiness."

Nothing first means "no thing in the comparison class"; second means "not having anything". Equivocation, see informal fallacies of presumption.

5. Necessary and sufficient conditions

When defining a concept, distinguish:

  • Necessary: without it, the concept doesn't apply. ("Being a mammal" is necessary for being a whale.)
  • Sufficient: having it guarantees the concept applies. ("Being a Bengal tiger" is sufficient for being a mammal.)

Many ordinary concepts (e.g. game, Wittgenstein) lack clean necessary-and-sufficient conditions — instead, they exhibit family resemblance.

6. Frequent failures

  • Reified abstractions: treating "the economy" or "society" as if it were a unified agent with intentions.
  • Persuasive definitions: smuggling a value judgment into a definition. "Democracy is the system that respects individual freedom" — packs a conclusion into a definition.
  • Loaded language: emotion-laden synonyms ("freedom fighter" vs "terrorist") preselect attitudes.

7. Example: pinning down a debate

"We should ban hate speech."

Before agreeing or disagreeing:

  • What counts as hate? (Lexical: dictionary; operational: legal threshold?)
  • What counts as speech? (Does symbolic action count?)
  • What does ban mean? (Criminal? Civil? Platform-level?)

A debate without these distinctions is not a debate, it's two people using the same words for different propositions.

Exercises

Identify the ambiguity: "Visiting relatives can be boring." Syntactic. Are *relatives* visiting (subject), or am I visiting them (object)?
Spot the equivocation: "Man is the only rational animal. No woman is a man. Therefore, no woman is rational." *Man* first means *human being* (gendered-neutral usage), then *male human*. Equivocation.

Summary

  • Three ambiguities: lexical, syntactic, scope. Different problems, different fixes.
  • Vagueness is not ambiguity — it's borderline cases without sharp lines.
  • Five definitions: lexical, stipulative, ostensive, genus-difference, operational.
  • Necessary vs sufficient: keep distinct.
  • Equivocation is a frequent root of fallacies — same word, two meanings, in one argument.

Further reading

  • Quine, Word and Object (1960).
  • Wittgenstein, Philosophical Investigations — on family resemblance.
  • Lakoff & Johnson, Metaphors We Live By.
