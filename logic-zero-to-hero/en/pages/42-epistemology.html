Epistemology: JTB, Gettier, intellectual virtues

What is knowledge? From the classical Platonic theory (justified true belief) to Gettier's counterexamples (1963) and contemporary responses: sensitivity, safety, reliabilism, virtue epistemology.

Area
Philosophy
Prerequisites
  • Have read [Anatomy of arguments](04-anatomy-of-arguments.html)
Tools / references
  • (none specific)

Epistemology studies knowledge: what it is, how it's acquired, what its limits are. For 2500 years the answer was "justified true belief". A three-page 1963 paper changed everything.

1. Classical theory: JTB (Justified True Belief)

Plato (Theaetetus 201d) approaches the idea: knowledge is "true opinion with logos". Modern form: $S$ knows that $p$ iff

  1. $p$ is true.
  2. $S$ believes $p$.
  3. $S$ is justified in believing $p$.

For over two millennia, nobody seriously challenged sufficiency.

2. Gettier's counterexamples (1963)

Edmund Gettier, "Is Justified True Belief Knowledge?", Analysis. Two pages, two examples that triggered 60 years of literature.

Case 1 (simplified)

Smith and Jones both apply for the same job. Smith has strong evidence that (a) Jones will get the job, and (b) Jones has 10 coins in his pocket. So Smith deduces: (c) the person who gets the job has 10 coins in their pocket.

In fact: Smith gets the job, and by coincidence Smith also has 10 coins in his pocket.

So (c) is true. Smith believed it. Justified by the deduction. But intuitively Smith doesn't know (c) — it's true by coincidence, not by the justification he had.

Pattern

Gettier cases: justification + true conclusion, but truth is disconnected from the justification. Telltale sign: justification passes through a false intermediate or an accidentally true path.

3. Responses

No false lemmas (Goldman 1967, first attempt)

JTB + no false premise used. Solves simple cases, fails subtler ones (fake-barn cases).

Sensitivity (Nozick 1981)

$S$ knows $p$ iff: JTB + if $p$ were false, $S$ wouldn't believe it.

Problem: anti-skeptical issues. Sensitivity makes some knowledge impossible.

Safety (Sosa 1999, Pritchard)

$S$ knows $p$ iff: in nearby possible worlds where $S$ believes $p$, $p$ is true. More robust variant.

Reliabilism (Goldman 1979)

Drop "justification" as internalist concept. $S$ knows iff belief is produced by a reliable cognitive process (good vision, valid deduction, accurate memory). Externalist: knowledge doesn't require self-aware justification, just that the generation process is reliable.

Virtue epistemology (Sosa, Zagzebski)

Knowledge is a performance of success due to a virtue of the subject. Like an archer "knows how to hit" if the shot is accurate (true), apt (justified), and due to skill (virtue), not wind (luck). Solves many Gettier cases: there the success is accidental.

4. Internalism vs externalism

  • Internalism: justification is internal to the agent's mind (introspection reveals it).
  • Externalism: justification can be external (e.g. process reliability).

Reliabilism is externalist. Cartesian tradition is internalist. Orthogonal to JTB, affects how we handle Gettier.

5. Classical skepticism

Pyrrhonists (3rd c. BCE)

Suspension of judgment (epoché): for any argument, an opposing one of equal force exists. Practical conclusion: live by appearances, no knowledge claims.

Descartes (Discourse on Method, 1637)

Methodical doubt: doubt everything until you find what can't be doubted. Result: cogito ergo sum. Reconstruct knowledge from there (problematically, requiring God).

Modern radical skepticism

"Brain in a vat" (Putnam 1981), Matrix scenarios: if you were a BIV, your experiences would be identical to reality; how do you know you're not? Responses: contextualism (DeRose), neo-Mooreanism ("I have a hand, so I'm not a BIV"), semantic externalism (BIV thoughts don't refer to the real world).

6. Foundationalism vs coherentism

  • Foundationalism: there's a layer of basic beliefs (self-evident, immediate, direct perception) on which everything rests. Descartes, some empiricists.
  • Coherentism: no foundation; beliefs support each other in a coherent web. BonJour, Quine. Critique of foundationalism: what justifies the basic beliefs?

Mixed views (e.g. Klein's infinitism) exist.

7. Intellectual virtues

Zagzebski (1996), Roberts & Wood (2007). Not just methods but character traits:

  • Open-mindedness: receptive to opposing evidence.
  • Intellectual humility: aware of one's limits.
  • Intellectual courage: defend unpopular but well-grounded views.
  • Curiosity: motivated to inquire.
  • Fair-mindedness: weigh views appropriately.
  • Perseverance: stay with difficulty.
  • Honesty: don't distort evidence.

Like Aristotelian moral virtues — habits, not switches.

Exercises

Build an original Gettier case. Maria looks at her friend's apartment window and sees the light on. She believes "Mario is home". Justified. Mario is in fact home. BUT: the light was on a timer, not because Mario is there. By coincidence, Mario is home sleeping. JTB ✓ but intuitively Maria doesn't *know* Mario is home.

Summary

  • Classical: JTB — 2500 years of consensus.
  • Gettier (1963): JTB insufficient.
  • Responses: no false lemmas, sensitivity, safety, reliabilism, virtue.
  • Internalism vs externalism on justification.
  • Skepticism: Pyrrhonists, Cartesian, BIV. No final resolution.
  • Intellectual virtues: open-mindedness, humility, courage, curiosity — cultivable.
