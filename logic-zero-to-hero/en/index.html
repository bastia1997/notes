Logic & Critical Thinking

From "what is an argument?" all the way to modal logic, Gödel's incompleteness theorems, and Pearl's do-calculus for causality. A long, ordered path, in English, covering formal logic, fallacies, cognitive biases, problem solving, decision theory, epistemology, rhetoric, science, paradoxes, systems thinking. University-grade material made chewable: every concept comes with intuition, formal treatment, a worked example (numerical or argumentative), and — where it matters — a warning about how people get it wrong or get manipulated.

53 sections Bilingual IT · EN Formulas + proofs Logic · Fallacies · Biases Bayes · Polya · Popper

How to use

Sections follow a logical order (pun intended): each chapter builds on the previous. You can jump to what you need (e.g. straight to "Informal fallacies" if you're getting ready for a debate), but if you want to actually understand why an argument can be "valid yet unsound", read the anatomy-of-arguments section first. Every page contains:

  • Theory — the conceptual "why", with references to philosophers/logicians/scientists where they help.
  • Formal treatment — symbols, rules, truth tables, proofs. KaTeX for formulas.
  • Examples — real arguments, mini-dialogues, numerical examples, step-by-step Bayesian / probabilistic calculations.
  • Charts — Mermaid diagrams and inline SVG (never ASCII art).
  • Exercises and questions — with hidden solutions inside <details> for self-assessment.
  • Traps and biases — where the mind misfires, and how to spot it.

Philosophy

Critical thinking is not "be skeptical of everything". It is distinguishing what is well-argued from what merely sounds convincing. The gap between someone who can reason well and someone who can't, over a lifetime, is enormous: it means not being swindled by an insurance contract, reading a scientific paper without taking the headline at face value, spotting a fallacy in a political speech, making medical or financial decisions on probabilistic grounds rather than anecdotes, and avoiding the trap of throwing years behind a wrong idea simply because you no longer know how to back out.

No "10 hacks to be rational". No lists of biases to memorise and then forget. Just the conceptual machinery — formal where needed, informal where it suffices — to think better. The rest is details — important, but details.

Who it's for

  • Anyone starting from zero who wants an ordered path from propositional logic to modal logic, no gaps.
  • Anyone who knows pieces (e.g. "I took a logic course once") but has holes (Bayesian decision theory, Gödel, Pearl's causality).
  • Anyone preparing for university exams in Logic, Philosophy of Science, Epistemology, Argumentation Theory, GRE, LSAT.
  • Anyone working with data, research, software, who wants to think with more rigour.
  • Anyone who simply wants to stop being fooled by rhetoricians, salespeople, and social media.

Who it's NOT for

  • Anyone looking for "IQ-superhero tricks". They don't exist. You think better with time and training, like with the body.
  • Anyone looking for confirmation of their worldview. Here you will find reasons to question it.

Recommended order

Follow sections in order. Estimated time for the full path: 4–8 months at 30–60 minutes per day. It's not a race. The "Reference" chapters (bilingual A-Z glossary, formula sheet, exercise book) are meant to be consulted back and forth at will.
