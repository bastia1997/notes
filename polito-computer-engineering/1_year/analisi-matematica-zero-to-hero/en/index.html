Mathematical Analysis I

From real numbers to Lebesgue integration, from Cauchy sequences to differential equations. A long, ordered path in English. Each chapter: concrete intuition, formal definition explained symbol by symbol, step-by-step proofs, numerical examples, exercises with hidden solutions, common pitfalls.

How to use

Sections are in logical order: each chapter builds on the previous one. You can jump to topics you need, but to really understand, read in sequence.

The path

00 · Preliminaries
What is mathematical analysis
A map of the path, the discipline, and the method. Explained for someone who has never heard of "limit", "derivative" or "integral" — starting from the concrete questions that gave birth to it all.
beginner
01 · Preliminaries
Logic and proofs for analysis
The symbols we need to write mathematics — ∀ ("for all"), ∃ ("there exists"), ¬ ("not"), and the four ways to prove a theorem. All explained symbol by symbol, with plain examples before the technical ones.
beginner
02 · Preliminaries
Sets, relations, functions
The vocabulary of analysis. What a set is, what a function is, what "injective" and "surjective" mean — all explained with everyday examples before the symbols, with a small inline glossary.
beginner
03 · Numbers
Natural numbers and the principle of induction
How to prove things "for infinitely many cases" using only two finite steps. What $\mathbb{N}$ is, what the Peano axioms say, and why induction is the "infinite domino chain" of mathematics.
beginner
04 · Numbers
Integers and rationals
How to "build" the integers $\mathbb{Z}$ and the rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ starting from the naturals $\mathbb{N}$. What a fraction really is, why $\sqrt 2$ isn't in $\mathbb{Q}$, and why rationals "aren't enough" to do analysis.
beginner
05 · Numbers
Cardinality and infinity (Cantor)
Are all infinities equal? No. Cantor discovered that $\mathbb{N}$ and $\mathbb{R}$ both have infinitely many elements, but $\mathbb{R}$ has "many more". We explain what "counting infinite sets" means, and the most elegant proof in all of mathematics (the diagonal).
intermediate
06 · Real numbers
Real numbers: the axioms
What are $\mathbb{R}$? Instead of constructing them (we'll do that in sec. 08), we **postulate** them, listing the rules that define them. Three families of rules — field, order, completeness — and everything we know about $\mathbb{R}$ follows.
intermediate
07 · Real numbers
Supremum and completeness
What's "the least upper bound" — the **sup** — and why it's the most powerful tool in analysis. Definition, $\varepsilon$-characterization (the "operational" form), difference from max, and equivalent forms of completeness of $\mathbb{R}$.
intermediate
08 · Real numbers
Construction of R: Dedekind and Cauchy
Building $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$. Two routes — **Dedekind cuts** (cuts on the rational line) and **Cauchy completion** (sequences that cluster). Both give the same $\mathbb{R}$.
advanced
09 · Complex numbers
Complex numbers
$\mathbb{C}$ is $\mathbb{R}$ with a new number $i$ satisfying $i^2 = -1$. What changes, why we need it, and how trigonometry simplifies into algebra through **Euler's formula** $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i \sin\theta$.
intermediate
10 · Topology
Topology of R: neighbourhoods, open, closed, compact
The formal grammar of "near" on $\mathbb{R}$. What a neighbourhood is, what an accumulation point is, what "open" and "closed" mean, and why **compact sets** (= closed + bounded) are the real protagonist of analysis.
intermediate
11 · Sequences
Sequences and limits: the ε-N definition
The **rigorous definition of a limit** of a sequence. Explained with very concrete examples before the symbols, with a glossary of every letter ($\varepsilon$, $N$, $a_n$). The single line all of analysis rests on.
intermediate
12 · Sequences
Algebra of sequence limits
The rules that let you compute limits **without** reapplying the ε-N definition every time. How sum, product, quotient behave — and the **indeterminate forms** where blind algebra fails.
intermediate
13 · Sequences
Monotone sequences and Bolzano-Weierstrass
Two cornerstone results. **Bounded monotone sequences converge** — tool to prove existence of limits without knowing them. **Bolzano-Weierstrass** — every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence. Both fail in $\mathbb{Q}$ and characterize completeness of $\mathbb{R}$.
intermediate
14 · Sequences
Cauchy sequences and completeness of R
The **internal** condition "terms get close to each other" — Cauchy sequence — is equivalent in $\mathbb{R}$ to convergence. The tool to prove **existence** of a limit **without knowing what it is**.
intermediate
15 · Sequences
Notable limits and Landau symbols (little-o, big-O)
The "atomic" limits that show up in a thousand forms — $(1 + 1/n)^n \to e$, $n^{1/n} \to 1$, the hierarchy $\log \ll n^\alpha \ll a^n \ll n!$ — and **Landau notation** ($o, O, \sim$) that lets you talk about "speeds" of infinity without computation.
intermediate
16 · Sequences
Limit superior, limit inferior, subsequences
Any sequence (even oscillating) **always** has two 'asymptotic shoulders' — $\liminf$ and $\limsup$ — the smallest and largest subsequence limits. The universal tool for talking about limits even when the 'real' limit doesn't exist.
advanced
17 · Elementary functions
Elementary functions: an overview
The "building blocks" of mathematics — polynomials, exponentials, logarithms, trigonometric, absolute value, integer part. Domain, image, parity, periodicity, monotonicity, composition, inverse. The operational vocabulary we'll use throughout the course.
beginner
18 · Elementary functions
Exponential, logarithm, powers
How to construct $a^x$ starting from integer $a^n$ and reaching arbitrary real exponent. The number $e$ as a limit. The **logarithm** as inverse of the exponential. Identities to memorize and symmetric graphs.
beginner
19 · Elementary functions
Trigonometric and hyperbolic functions
The functions of oscillation ($\sin, \cos, \tan$) built from the unit circle. The **hyperbolic** ($\sinh, \cosh, \tanh$) — algebraic cousins via $e^x$. Identities, graphs, inverses.
beginner
20 · Limits
Limit of a function: the definition
The $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition of "$f(x) \to L$ as $x \to x_0$" — the rigorous translation of "gets close". Versions with one-sided limits, at infinity, infinite. Heine's sequential characterization.
intermediate
21 · Limits
Theorems on limits of functions
Algebraic rules to compute limits **without** going back to the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition every time. Uniqueness, sum/product/quotient, sign persistence, **squeeze theorem**, substitution, indeterminate forms.
intermediate
22 · Limits
Notable limits for functions
The 'atomic' limits underlying calculus with exponentials, logarithms, trigonometric. $\sin x/x \to 1$, $(e^x - 1)/x \to 1$, $\ln(1+x)/x \to 1$, $(1+x)^{1/x} \to e$, and the rest. Proved carefully, applied with standard techniques.
intermediate
23 · Limits
Horizontal, vertical, oblique asymptotes
The **lines** a function's graph approaches "indefinitely". Definitions of the three types, formulas for slope $m$ and intercept $q$ of obliques, and the algorithm to find all asymptotes.
intermediate
24 · Continuity
Continuity: definition and first properties
The $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition of continuity — the "no jumps" condition — and what changes from a limit. Heine's sequential characterization, algebra of continuous functions, composition, and guiding examples (Dirichlet nowhere continuous, $x\sin(1/x)$ extendable).
intermediate
25 · Continuity
Theorems on continuous functions (Weierstrass, intermediate values)
The **three pillars** on continuous functions of a real variable. **Bolzano** (zeros), **Darboux** (intermediate values), **Weierstrass** (max/min on compacts). All depend on completeness of $\mathbb{R}$.
intermediate
26 · Continuity
Uniform continuity, Heine, Lipschitz
**Uniform continuity** — same $\delta$ for all points, not just 'one $\delta$ per point'. Swapping quantifier order produces a **strictly stronger** notion. Heine-Cantor theorem (compact ⇒ uniform), Lipschitz and Hölder.
intermediate
27 · Continuity
Types of discontinuities
Three types of isolated discontinuities — **removable** (limit exists, $\ne f(x_0)$), **jump** (different one-sided limits), **essential** (a one-sided limit missing or infinite). Monotone functions have only jumps, at most countably many.
intermediate
28 · Derivatives
Derivative: definition and geometric interpretation
The definition of the **derivative** as a limit of difference quotients — the instantaneous slope of a curve. Geometric meaning (tangent), physical (velocity). Differentiable ⇒ continuous, but not conversely ($|x|$ at 0).
intermediate
29 · Derivatives
Differentiation rules (sum, product, quotient, chain)
Algebraic rules for differentiating combinations — sum, product (Leibniz), quotient, **chain rule**, derivative of inverse. All proved from the definition, applied daily.
intermediate
30 · Derivatives
Derivatives of elementary functions
The **table of fundamental derivatives** — the core vocabulary of calculus. Every entry proved, from the power rule to inverse trig. Memorize, don't redo the limit each time.
intermediate
31 · Derivatives
Differential calculus theorems: Fermat, Rolle, Lagrange, Cauchy
The **four theorems** turning the derivative from local object into a global tool. **Fermat** (interior extrema → $f' = 0$), **Rolle** (equal at endpoints → $f' = 0$ somewhere), **Lagrange** (mean value), **Cauchy** (two-function generalization).
intermediate
32 · Derivatives
L'Hôpital's rule
The most-used (and most-abused) tool for $0/0$ and $\infty/\infty$ forms — differentiate numerator and denominator separately and retry. Works when hypotheses hold; fails when they're forgotten.
intermediate
33 · Derivatives
Taylor polynomial and Taylor's formula
Approximating $f$ by a polynomial of degree $n$ near a point. **Taylor's formula** with Peano, Lagrange, integral remainders. Notable Maclaurin expansions of $e^x, \sin x, \cos x, \ln(1+x), (1+x)^\alpha$.
intermediate
34 · Derivatives
Computing limits with Taylor expansions
The most powerful technique for $0/0$ limits with elementary functions. Replace each $f$ with its Taylor polynomial of sufficient order, do the algebra, read the limit from the dominant term.
intermediate
35 · Derivatives
Monotonicity, maxima, minima, critical points
From **sign of $f'$** to monotonicity. First and second derivative tests for max/min/inflection. Tools for function studies and optimization.
intermediate
36 · Derivatives
Convexity, concavity, inflection points
The **global curvature** of a function. **Convex** = chords above graph ("valley shape"). Equivalent definitions (geometric, $f'' \ge 0$, Jensen). Inflection points, classical inequalities.
intermediate
37 · Derivatives
Complete curve sketching
The canonical procedure to draw the graph of $f(x)$ — domain, symmetries, sign, asymptotes, monotonicity, concavity. The "synthesis" of all differential calculus seen so far.
intermediate
38 · Applications
Optimization applications: physics, economics, geometry
Max/min problems from the real world solved with derivatives. The uniform recipe and three classical examples (optimal can, Snell's law, max profit).
intermediate
39 · Integrals
The Riemann integral: definition
Rigorous construction of the **integral** as "area under the curve". Upper/lower sums, integrability criterion, integrability of continuous and monotone functions, properties (linearity, monotonicity, additivity, mean-value theorem).
intermediate
40 · Integrals
Fundamental theorem of calculus
The **bridge** between derivative and integral. **FTC-I:** the integral function has the integrand as derivative. **FTC-II (Newton-Leibniz):** $\int_a^b f = F(b) - F(a)$. Table of immediate integrals.
intermediate
41 · Integrals
Integration techniques: parts, substitution
The **two great techniques**. **Parts** = transpose of product rule, $\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$. **Substitution** = transpose of chain rule, $\int f(\varphi)\varphi'\,dx = \int f(u)\,du$. Standard substitutions.
intermediate
42 · Integrals
Integration of rational functions
**Deterministic algorithm** to integrate any $P(x)/Q(x)$: euclidean division, factorization of $Q$, **partial fractions**, term-by-term integration. Result always elementary (Liouville).
intermediate
43 · Integrals
Improper integrals
Extension of Riemann to unbounded intervals (**1st kind**) and unbounded functions (**2nd kind**) via **limit**. $p$-test, comparison, asymptotic comparison, integral test. Absolute convergence. Euler's $\Gamma$ function.
intermediate
44 · Integrals
Integral functions and geometric applications
Study of $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$ as a function (monotonicity, concavity, extrema). **Geometric applications:** area between curves, volumes (disks, shells, Pappus-Guldinus), arc length, surface of revolution.
intermediate
45 · Series
Numerical series: definition and convergence
Summing **infinitely many** terms = limit of partial sums. **Geometric** (closed form), **harmonic** (diverges slowly), **generalized harmonic**. Necessary condition $a_n \to 0$. Telescoping, Cauchy criterion.
intermediate
46 · Series
Convergence tests for positive series
For $a_n \ge 0$, convergence $\iff$ partial sums bounded. Catalog of tests: **comparison**, **asymptotic comparison**, **ratio** (D'Alembert), **root** (Cauchy), **condensation**, **integral**.
intermediate
47 · Series
Variable-sign series, Leibniz, absolute convergence
For variable-sign terms, positive criteria don't suffice. **Leibniz** for alternating, **absolute convergence** (stronger than simple), **Riemann** on rearrangements, **Dirichlet** and **Abel**.
intermediate
48 · Series
Power series and radius of convergence
$\sum a_n (x-x_0)^n$. Always a **radius** $R \in [0, +\infty]$: converges for $|x-x_0|<R$, diverges for $|x-x_0|>R$. **Cauchy-Hadamard**, term-by-term derivation/integration, boundaries case by case.
intermediate
49 · Series
Taylor series and analyticity
For $f \in C^\infty$ near $x_0$, the **Taylor series** is $\sum f^{(n)}(x_0)(x-x_0)^n/n!$. Converges to $f$ $\iff R_n \to 0$. **Analytic** functions, classical expansions, the celebrated $C^\infty$ non-analytic counterexample.
advanced
50 · Series
Fourier series: introduction
Decomposing **periodic** functions into harmonics $\{1, \cos nx, \sin nx\}$. Coefficients, convergence (Dirichlet, $L^2$), **Parseval**, **Gibbs** phenomenon. The gateway to harmonic analysis.
advanced
51 · ODEs
First-order ODEs
$y' = f(x,y)$. **Separable** (decay, logistic). **Linear** with **integrating factor** $\mu = e^{\int a}$. Bernoulli, exact equations, direction fields.
intermediate
52 · ODEs
Linear ODEs of order $n$ with constant coefficients
Homogeneous: **characteristic polynomial**, solution structure (distinct real roots, multiple, complex). Complete: **undetermined coefficients**, **variation of parameters**, **resonance**. Wronskian.
intermediate
53 · ODEs
Cauchy problem, existence and uniqueness
Cauchy problem = IVP. **Picard-Lindelöf** (Cauchy-Lipschitz) via contraction + iteration. **Peano** (continuity alone). Counterexample $y' = \sqrt{|y|}$. Extension, **Gronwall**, continuous dependence.
advanced
54 · R^n
Topology of $\mathbb{R}^n$
Euclidean norm, **Cauchy-Schwarz**, triangle. Open, closed, boundary, accumulation. **Heine-Borel** (compact = closed + bounded). Path-connectedness. Sequences and completeness.
intermediate
55 · R^n
Partial derivatives and differential in several variables
**Partial derivatives**, **gradient** $\nabla f$, directional derivative. **Differentiability** (linear approximation exists). Total differential theorem, chain, **Schwarz**, **Hessian**, free max/min.
advanced
56 · Advanced
Beyond Riemann: Lebesgue, Banach, Hilbert
Limits of Riemann and Dirichlet's function. **Lebesgue** (measures the codomain). $L^p$ spaces and **Banach**. **Hilbert** spaces, orthogonality, orthonormal bases, **Parseval**, **Riesz**. Why it matters.
advanced
